Critical Pattern #3: Jumping to Solutions

We solve problems before we understand them. This makes us look like order-takers, not strategic partners.

‚ùå John Call #1: The Workflow Trap

Line 45-52
John: "My GHL workflows are a mess. I need someone to clean them up."
Prakarsh: "Yeah, we can definitely help with that. Let me get access to your GHL and I'll audit it."

What happened: We immediately offered to solve the stated problem.
What we missed: WHY are the workflows a mess? What business problem does this create? Is this even the real issue?

‚úÖ What We Should Have Said: The 3-Why Discovery Method

Prakarsh: "Before we dive into your workflows, help me understand the business context. What revenue milestone are you trying to hit right now?"
John: "I want to get to $50K/month."
Prakarsh: "Got it. And right now you're at...?"
John: "About $15K."
Prakarsh: "What's stopping you from hitting $50K? Why do you think messy workflows are the bottleneck versus, say, not enough traffic or poor conversion rates?"

Now you're diagnosing, not just executing. This is what "growth partners" do.

üí° The "Why x3" Framework

  1. First Why: What's the business goal? (Revenue, funding, scaling)
  2. Second Why: What's blocking that goal? (Get them to articulate the real problem)
  3. Third Why: Why is THAT the blocker versus alternatives? (Challenge their assumptions)

‚ö†Ô∏è WARNING: If you can't articulate the client's business problem in one sentence after discovery, you didn't do enough discovery.

üí° Objection Handling Framework: Feel-Felt-Found

1. FEEL: "I hear you" / "I understand that concern"
‚Üí Validates their objection, builds rapport

‚Üí Shows you're not defensive, they're not alone

3. FOUND: "Here's what we've found works / how we're different"
‚Üí Now you can reframe without sounding like you're making excuses

  • Budget: "What's your budget for solving this problem?" Asked: 0/5 calls
  • Urgency: "What happens if you don't solve this in 6 months?" Asked: 0/5 calls
